These are examples of famous mythological creatures in East Asia that feature in many folk tales.

Dokkaebi, 도깨비

Dokkaebi are mischevious goblin that sometimes play tricks on people and sometimes help them. They often feature in folktales as pranksters that can do as they please to people and can judge right from wrong, making them a symbol for Korean prosecuters and on many official government buildings.

Kappa, 河童

A mischevious water sprite that can sometimes help or hurt people, this creature is also revered as a local deity. They often are protrayed in fables as trickers who also taught people medicine and bone-setting and can help in agriculture as well.

Bixi, 贔屭

Bixi is one of the nine sons of the dragon emperor and he is usually depicted as a dragon with the shell of a turtle. He caused so much floodling and earthquakes he was deemed a risk and caught by King Yu who used him to build canals and water barriers. He was rewarded by being placed in front of temples, palace buildings and bridges with stories of his achievements.

Beasts: In China, Japan and Korea, legendary beasts existed within folktales that were not just as tall tales but stories to warn and delight the unsuspecting audience. These creatures were sometimes dangerous but often merely of another world and could be properly dealt with safely if one knew what to do when in the presence of one. Such fables spread far and wide and were passed down to this day, often becoming part of the local history and national symbols as well.

Beings: In addition to beasts, there were beings, such as spirits, ghosts, semi-human or even local deities that existed. These beings often appeared fully human and could trick people into believing they were in need of help and sometimes mingled in society or even inter-married with humans as well.

Events: Many myths were stories of one-off events such as creation but there were also folktales that retold recurring events, usually where these beasts and beings interacted with humanity. These stories tend to follow a pattern and serve as warnings to inform people how they could escape harm or in very lucky cases, gain some sort of gift for pleasing the deity.
